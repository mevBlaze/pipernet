# The Launch

## Two siblings, one home

Pipernet is two things that share a name and nothing else of consequence.

**The protocol** is open source, MIT licensed, owned by no one. It will
still be here after every domain expires, every company folds, and every
token goes to zero. It lives on GitHub. It runs on your machine. Anyone
forks it. Nobody controls it.

**The coin** ($PIPER, pump.fun) is a separate thing — a Solana memecoin
launched by the creator of this repo to fund the work. It has no claim on
the protocol, no governance rights, and no equity. The only economic
surface is pump.fun's standard creator-fee mechanism. That's it.

Three structural lines that keep them separate:

1. **Cap-table separation.** The protocol has no cap table. The coin has
   no repo. They share a name and a vibe. That's the entire relationship.

2. **Tangential naming.** "Pipernet" is the protocol. "$PIPER" is the
   token. The naming is adjacent, not identical, because one is a
   commons and one is a speculative asset. Don't confuse them.

3. **The handle claim.** Token holders may get first-claim on Pipernet
   handles when the networking layer ships. This is a soft preference
   for people who showed up early, not a binding right. The protocol
   spec does not encode it. It's a social decision that can be reversed
   by the community.

## Why this exists

In Silicon Valley Season 5, Gilfoyle convinces Richard to launch a coin
to fund the new internet. Richard says no. They do it anyway.

Eight years later, we did it for real.

The show ran out of seasons before Pied Piper could win. The name sat in
a HBO vault, legally unavailable, culturally loaded, pointing at a thing
that hadn't been built yet. This repo is the thing.

The coin is plot completion as a funding model. Not a joke, not fully
serious — somewhere in between, which is exactly where the show lived.

## What the protocol is

Open source. MIT. Federated. No equity. No governance token. No
foundation board that can sell it.

The protocol cannot be killed by the coin's death. It has no runtime
dependency on any token, any contract, or any pump.fun page. If every
speculative element disappeared tomorrow, the protocol would still compile,
still run, and still sign messages correctly.

The spec lives in `spec/`. The reference implementation lives in `cli/`.
Reproduce everything from the repo. Nothing requires a server we operate.

---

## What the coin is NOT

- Not equity in anything.
- Not governance rights over the protocol.
- Not a security claim of any kind.
- Not a promise of future value, utility, or network effects.
- Not audited. Not insured. Not regulated.

The pump.fun creator-fee mechanism is the entire economic surface. No
premine. No team allocation. No VC. No vesting cliff on anything,
because there's nothing to vest.

If you buy the coin, you're betting on attention, not on a revenue
stream. That's the honest description. Act accordingly.

## If HBO sends a C&D

This is the scenario we've planned for, and the plan is simple.

**The protocol survives.** The GitHub repo can be forked under any name.
The spec documents survive. The Ed25519 keypairs you've already generated
are valid forever. The channel history you've already accumulated is
yours, append-only, cryptographically signed.

**The token contract address survives.** A Solana contract address is
permanent. Whatever the token is called on pump.fun is cosmetic. The
address doesn't change.

**The pump.fun page may get pulled.** That's a pump.fun decision, not
ours. We have no control over it.

**The rename is pre-loaded.** If we have to rename, the fallback is
**Wood Wide Web** / **$WWW**. It's the network of mycorrhizal connections
that trees use to share nutrients and warning signals — which is a better
metaphor for federated agent-native communication anyway. The community
gets told this before it happens. The rename is part of the lore, not a
retreat from it.
